# Example blog 3 — "When Should Children See a GP?" (The Reassurer GP, female voice)

There is no parenting milestone that tests your confidence quite like deciding whether your sick child needs a doctor.
Too soon and you feel like you're overreacting. Too late and you feel like you missed something. There is no comfortable middle ground when it's your child.
I've had this conversation with hundreds of parents. Here's how I think about it.
When should a child see a GP?
The honest answer is: when you're worried. Your instinct as a parent carries real information, and "something doesn't feel right" is a legitimate reason to book an appointment. You are not wasting anyone's time.
That said, here's a practical guide to the situations that generally warrant a GP visit — and the ones that can usually be managed at home.
Book a GP appointment when:

Your child has had a fever above 38°C for more than 48 hours
They have an earache that isn't improving, or they're pulling at their ear repeatedly
A cough has lasted more than two weeks
They've had diarrhoea or vomiting for more than 24 hours and aren't keeping fluids down
A rash appears suddenly, especially with a fever
They seem unusually lethargic or difficult to rouse
You've tried the usual things and nothing is working
You're worried

Managing at home is usually fine when:

It's a mild cold with a runny nose and no fever
There's a low-grade temperature (under 38°C) and the child is drinking and alert
They have a minor cut or graze that's clean and not deep

When to go straight to emergency:
Some situations need emergency care rather than a GP appointment. Go directly to emergency if your child is having difficulty breathing, has a high fever with a rash that doesn't fade when you press it (press a glass against it — if the rash is still visible through the glass, go now), has a febrile seizure, is unresponsive or very difficult to wake, or has swallowed something potentially dangerous.
What about after hours?
Woodlands Family Practice offers access to after-hours care through Night Doctor for situations that can't wait until the next available appointment. It's worth knowing this exists before you need it.
The bit I always say
Childhood illness is one of the most stressful experiences of parenting, and one of the most normal. Most of what I see is viral — it's going to resolve, your child is going to be fine, and there was nothing you could have done differently.
But if you're not sure, come in. Seeing a GP when you didn't strictly need to is always better than the alternative.
FAQ
When should I take my child to a GP vs emergency in Perth?
GPs are for non-urgent concerns — fever, ear infections, coughs, rashes without other serious symptoms. Emergency is for breathing difficulty, unresponsiveness, severe injury, or a rash that doesn't fade under pressure.
How do I know if my child's fever is serious?
A fever above 38°C in a child under 3 months warrants an urgent GP or emergency visit. In older children, duration and behaviour matters more than the number — a child with a 39°C fever who is alert, drinking, and responsive is less concerning than a child with 38°C who is unusually difficult to rouse.
