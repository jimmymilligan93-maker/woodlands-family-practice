# Statistics guide — WFP blogs

## The rule of three
Every statistic must follow this structure:
1. Stat — the number
2. Plain English — what it actually means
3. So what — why the reader should care

Never lead with a number. The number is the evidence, not the hook.

## Format example
WRONG: "73% of Australians will experience a mental health condition in their lifetime."

RIGHT: "Mental health conditions aren't rare — roughly three in four Australians 
will experience one at some point. That means it's almost certainly affected 
someone in your household. Which is exactly why there's no shame in asking 
your GP about a Mental Health Care Plan."

## WA-specific stats to use (verified, cite source in blog)
- WA has Australia's highest rate of melanoma diagnosis.
- 2 in 3 Australians will be diagnosed with skin cancer by age 70.
- Only 54% of Australians with a mental health condition seek professional help.
- Medicare covers up to 10 individual sessions with a psychologist per year 
  under a Mental Health Care Plan.
- Cervical cancer is one of the most preventable cancers — screening reduces 
  risk by up to 90%.
- Adults aged 18–64 should do at least 150 minutes of moderate physical 
  activity per week (Australian guidelines).

## Style rules
- Round numbers unless precision is the point ("roughly 1 in 3", not "32.7%")
- Always name the source briefly: "(Australian Institute of Health and Welfare)", 
  not a full citation
- Never stack two statistics in the same paragraph
- After every stat, write at least two sentences of human context before 
  the next stat
- Avoid statistics that are more than 5 years old
