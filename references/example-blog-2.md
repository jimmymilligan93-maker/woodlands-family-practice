# Example blog 2 — "What SPF Sunscreen Should I Use in Perth?" (The Pragmatist GP, male voice)

Here's something I tell almost every patient who comes in for a skin check: I can see exactly which part of your arm sticks out from your car window.
Perth drivers are getting sun damage on one side only. Left arm, left side of the neck, sometimes the left side of the face. It's one of the most common patterns I see in the clinic.
Sunscreen isn't complicated. But most people are using it wrong, not using enough of it, or using the wrong SPF entirely. Let me fix that.
What SPF sunscreen should you use in Perth?
In Western Australia, the answer is SPF50+ — every day, not just at the beach. Perth receives some of the highest UV radiation levels in the world. The UV index regularly exceeds 10 (classified as "extreme") during summer, and even on overcast winter days it can reach 3–4, which is enough to cause cumulative skin damage over time.
SPF30 filters about 97% of UVB radiation. SPF50+ filters about 98%. That 1% difference sounds minor until you factor in daily exposure over 20 years.
Does the brand matter?
Not much. What matters far more is the application — most people use about 25% of the amount they actually need. The general rule is one teaspoon for the face and neck, one teaspoon per limb, and one teaspoon for each the front and back of the torso.
Apply 20 minutes before going outside. Reapply every two hours if you're outdoors, or after swimming or sweating.
Any broad-spectrum SPF50+ from a pharmacy will do the job. The best sunscreen is the one you'll actually use consistently.
What about vitamin D?
This comes up a lot. Yes, your skin produces vitamin D from sunlight. No, you don't need unprotected sun exposure to maintain adequate levels — incidental sun exposure through daily activity is usually sufficient for most people, especially in Perth.
If you're concerned about your vitamin D levels, ask for a blood test at your next appointment. It's a simple test and it gives you an actual answer rather than guesswork.
When should you see a GP about your skin?
Two in three Australians will be diagnosed with skin cancer by age 70 (Cancer Council Australia). WA has the highest melanoma rate in the country. Those numbers aren't meant to alarm you — they're meant to make the case for regular skin checks.
If you notice a new spot, a spot that's changing shape or colour, something that bleeds, itches, or won't heal — book a skin check. Don't wait until your next annual check.
And if you haven't had a skin check in the last two years and you live in Perth? Book one. Fifteen minutes. Done.
FAQ
What SPF should I use in Perth daily?
SPF50+ broad-spectrum, applied daily — not just at the beach. Year-round in WA.
Can I get a skin check at Woodlands Family Practice?
Yes. We offer skin checks in Woodlands. Book online or call the clinic.
How often should I have a professional skin check in Western Australia?
Once a year is the general recommendation for adults in WA. More frequently if you have a personal or family history of skin cancer, fair skin, or significant lifetime sun exposure.
Does sunscreen stop vitamin D production?
Largely yes, but most people get adequate incidental UV exposure for vitamin D through daily activity. If you're concerned, ask your GP for a simple blood test.
